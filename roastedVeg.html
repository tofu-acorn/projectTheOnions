Roasted Vegetables with Wild Rice and Caramelized Onions

Use any grain you want. If you don't have any leftover grains but still want one, you will have about 45 minutes until the vegetables are finished, and that time does not include prep or preheating. I use wild rice here, and depending on the variety, it takes about 1.5 hours to cook, so it was leftover. Quinoa, on the other hand, takes about 20 minutes after the water boils.

Regarding which vegetables to choose, I can't think of a single vegetable that doesn't benefit from roasting, so please use whatever you like. Just keep in mind that you'll need to mix and match times. Asparagus only takes about 15-25 minutes (depending on the thickness), while the large carrots I use here cooked for 45 minutes. Be aware that the cutting the vegetables can make a big difference, such as roasting potato wedges compared to baking a whole potato. Just google the vegetable, note the time, and to be extra safe, check on the vegetables while they are cooking: 15 minutes for delicate ones like asparagus or frozen thinly sliced peppers, 25-30 for heartier vegetables like carrots. Use a fork or tongs to pull a bit of the vegetable out of the oven and inspect it. Once you've practiced roasting vegetables several times, you'll begin to get a feel for it. Don't be afraid to open that oven door, a few extra minutes of cook time from temperature loss is preferable to tossing out burnt vegetables.

Ingredients

  • 4 carrots, raw
  • 1-2 onions, raw
  • 1 package (around 14 oz) broccoli florets, frozen
  • 1 package (around 14 oz) cauliflower florets, frozen
  • Canola oil, to coat vegetables
  • Salt Sea salt has a more mellow flavor than kosher or iodized, but just use what you have on hand
  • 1 cup - 2 cups leftover grain, such as rice or quinoa
  • 1/2 cup - 1 cup caramelized onions


Instructions

  1. Preheat oven to 400 degrees, and line two sheet trays (better known as baking sheets, or cookie sheets) with aluminum foil. You can also cook directly on the sheet trays, but it's a mess to clean up.
  2. Carrot prep: cut the hard nub off of the top of the carrot, and then leave the carrot whole, and place on one side of a foiled sheet tray. If the greens are still attached, here is an article of how they can be used instead of tossed.
  3. Broccoli and Cauliflower prep: open both bags and dump one out on the left side of a foiled pan, and one out on the right side. Remove any big pieces of ice, and if there are lots of small ice bits, don't worry about them. They will just evaporate in the oven. Try to keep the broccoli and cauliflower seperate because, depending upon the manufacturer, broccoli florets can sometimes be small, and they might need to be removed with a spatula or tongs before the cauliflower is finished. See the note on frozen broccoli below if you accidently bought chopped broccoli.
  4. Onion prep: cut both ends off the onion, peel, and place one of the cut sides down on the cutting board. Slice through east-to-west, and then north-south, for four equal portions. If it's a very large onion (like getting close to soft-ball size) you may need to make two more cuts, this time diagonally, for 8 equal pieces. Try to pick the whole onion up as one solid piece, and then set it down on one side of a foiled sheet tray.
  5. I use disposable gloves for this step, but you can always just wash your hands before and after. Pour some oil over the carrots and the top of the onion, and then over the frozen broccoli and cauliflower. Pick up each carrot and oil it from top to bottom, then move onto the onions. Rub the oil on the top, and on the rounded sides, and then move the whole onion around on the foil to get some of the oil on the bottom of the onion too. Now flip each onion piece over on its rounded edge, so that the thin tip points toward the ceiling. Run your oiled fingers on both sides of each piece, taking care to not break them up. If they start to break apart then you can restack them, but just do your best, and if they fall completely apart then just cut them larger next time. As for the broccoli and cauliflower, just work your hands around them like you're giving them a massage. It's difficult to actually see how much oil is on them because on the ice, but if you massage them they will get coated.
  6. Take off your gloves/wash your hands and sprinkle salt over the vegetables. A very light sprinkle is just fine, maybe about 1/4 teaspoon. You only need the salt on one side, so place both sheet trays in the oven. I only have one rack in my oven so I place them side by side, and if this is your situation also, but they don't seem to fit, try to push the rack back against the oven and that should give you a little more clearance.
  7. Set the timer for 25 minutes, to be extra safe (see explanation above and pictures below) and your vegetables are roasted when they look like the picture below. For my oven, it was 45 minutes.
  8. Mix your carmelized onions and leftover grain, and warm them however you'd like (stovetop, microwave, ect.) right before the vegetables are ready. Portion everything onto plates or in a bowl, and serve.

  9. NOTE ON FROZEN BROCCOLI If you accidently bought chopped broccoli instead of florets, and the pieces are really tiny, refoil the sheet tray with two seperate foil squares that fit side by side. Just use the inside edges of the sheet tray to shape both pieces of foil so that all four of their edges stick up at least 1/2 inch. This will help avoid any oil spills. Chopped frozen broccoli won't last much longer than 25-30 minutes in the oven, so you'll need to pull it out of the oven around that time. To do this, set the whole sheet tray on the stove top, grab one corner of the foil square with tongs or a pot holder, and slide the foil carefully onto a plate. Put the sheet tray back in the oven so the cauliflower can finish cooking.
